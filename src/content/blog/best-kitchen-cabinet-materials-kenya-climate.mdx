The most beautiful kitchen in the world is worthless if it swells, peels or sags within a couple of years. In Kenya — with our humidity and, on the coast, real salt-laden air — material choice is everything. Here's what I specify to make sure a kitchen lasts, and why.

## The carcass: where it starts

The carcass is the box that everything else hangs on, so it has to be stable in humidity. I use moisture-resistant boards rather than ordinary chipboard, because standard board absorbs moisture, swells and loses its grip on screws and hinges over time. A stable carcass keeps doors aligned and drawers running true for years.

## Door fronts that hold up

Door fronts take the most handling and the most exposure to steam and splashes. Quality laminate and well-finished fronts resist moisture and clean easily. Whatever the finish, what matters is that it's properly sealed and the surface won't lift at the edges.

## Edge-banding: the unsung hero

This is where cheap kitchens fail first. If the edges of the board aren't sealed with quality edge-banding, moisture creeps in and the door starts to swell and peel along the rim. I insist on durable, well-bonded edge-banding on every panel — it's invisible when done right and disastrous when skipped.

## Worktops for daily life

Worktops need to handle heat, water and constant use. The right choice balances durability, looks and budget — and crucially, it must be sealed properly around the sink and hob, where water damage usually begins. I help clients choose a worktop that suits how they cook and how much maintenance they want.

## Hardware that won't rust

Hinges and runners live in a humid environment and get used constantly. I fit quality, corrosion-resistant soft-close hardware so drawers and doors keep working smoothly. Cheap hardware rusts, sags and starts to stick — and it's miserable to live with.

## Protecting against humidity day to day

Beyond materials, good design helps: proper extraction over the hob reduces steam, and a small gap for airflow behind units prevents trapped moisture. These details extend the life of even the best materials.

## Coast vs inland

If you're on the coast, the salt air is even harsher, so I lean toward the most moisture- and corrosion-resistant specifications available. Inland in Nairobi, the priority is humidity resistance and quality sealing.

## Built to last is built once

Spending a little more on the right materials means you build your kitchen once, not twice. That's always my approach.
