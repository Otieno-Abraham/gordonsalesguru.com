A showroom visit is the best way to turn vague ideas into a real plan. You get to see and touch the materials, picture the finishes in your own home, and ask everything face to face. To help you make the most of your visit to my showroom on Mombasa Road, here's how to prepare.

## Bring photos of your space

The single most useful thing you can bring is photos of the room — the whole space and any tricky corners, plus the door, window and where water and power come in. Photos let me give you genuinely relevant advice rather than generic suggestions, and they help you visualise finishes against your actual walls and floor.

## Bring rough measurements

You don't need professional precision — wall lengths and ceiling height are enough to start a meaningful conversation about layout and what's possible. (I'll take exact measurements on site later.) Even ballpark numbers make the visit far more productive.

## Know your priorities

Have a think beforehand about what matters most to you: maximum storage, a particular look, easy cleaning, durability, a tight timeline. When you tell me your priorities, I can steer you toward the right choices quickly instead of overwhelming you with options.

## Gather inspiration

Save a few images of kitchens, wardrobes or bathrooms you love — and, just as helpfully, a couple you don't. Knowing what you want to avoid is as useful as knowing what you like. It helps me understand your taste fast.

## Come ready to touch everything

The whole point of a showroom is the things you can't judge online — how a finish feels, how solid the hardware is, how a drawer glides, how colours look in real light. Open doors, pull out drawers, run your hand over the surfaces. This is how you tell quality from a pretty picture.

## Ask the important questions

Don't hold back. Ask about materials and moisture resistance, what hardware is standard, what's included in a quote, realistic timelines, and after-sales support. A good visit should leave you with clear, honest answers and no lingering doubts.

## Talk budget openly

Sharing your budget isn't awkward — it's helpful. It lets me show you what's achievable and design something realistic from the start, rather than falling in love with something out of reach.

## Leave with a clear next step

By the end of your visit, you should have a direction, a sense of what's possible, and an agreed next step — usually a site measure and a tailored quote.

## Plan your visit

I recommend booking ahead so I can set aside time to give you my full attention.
