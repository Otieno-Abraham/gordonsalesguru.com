For a property developer, the interior fitout is where a building becomes a home that sells. Kitchens, wardrobes and bathrooms are the spaces buyers and tenants judge most — and getting them right across an entire development is both an art and a logistics exercise. This is my complete guide for Nairobi developers.

## Start with your target buyer

Every specification decision should flow from who you're selling or letting to. A premium apartment block aimed at executives demands a different finish from an affordable housing scheme. Define the buyer first, then specify fitouts that hit their expectations without over- or under-spending. This single discipline protects your margins.

## Specify for consistency

In a development, consistency is everything. The kitchen in the last unit must match the show home exactly. That requires a partner who can reproduce the same quality, finish and detail across dozens of units — not one who delivers a beautiful show kitchen and then drops off. I build to a fixed specification so every unit lands identically.

## Balance cost and appeal

The developer's tightrope is delivering interiors that impress buyers while keeping the cost per unit viable. The trick is investing where buyers look and touch — the kitchen, the wardrobes, quality hardware — and being efficient everywhere else. I help developers specify fitouts that photograph well, show beautifully, and stay within budget.

## Plan around the construction programme

A fitout that arrives late stalls handover, sales and income. I plan production and installation to slot into your build schedule, sequence units sensibly, and keep you updated at every milestone. Predictable delivery is as valuable to a developer as the product itself.

## Prioritise durability

Across many units, durability isn't just about quality — it's about avoiding costly call-backs and protecting your reputation. I use moisture-resistant materials and quality hardware throughout, built for Kenya's climate, so the units hold up long after residents move in and your name stays strong.

## Design for the way people live

Smart, generous storage sells homes. Well-planned kitchens, full-height wardrobes and clutter-free bathrooms make units feel bigger and more livable in a viewing. These are the details that turn a "nice" apartment into one a buyer commits to.

## Keep it simple: one accountable partner

Developers manage enough moving parts. Working with a single, accountable point of contact for all cabinetry and fitout work removes a huge headache — one brief, one standard, one person who owns the outcome from specification to handover and after-sales.

## A partnership, not a transaction

The best developer relationships are long-term. Deliver one scheme well — on time, on spec, on budget — and the next one is easier for everyone. That's the way I like to work.
